# The Line in the Mind

*"The map is not the territory, but the map is also a weapon."* — anonymous

---

## A Man Picks His Own Birthday

Somewhere in the Napa Valley, in a summer that might have been 1938 or might have been 1941, a grown man sat down with Ursula Le Guin's father and picked out a day to have been born on. His name was Juan Dolores. He was Papago. He came up every June for his visit, beat the Kroeber children at croquet, and stayed long enough that the upstairs bedroom is still called Juan's Room by people in the family who are now old themselves. The problem was paperwork. You cannot have a pension, or a social security number, or a line on a bureaucrat's form, without a birthday. So Juan and Alfred Kroeber sat down and picked one. They picked Midsummer Night, the eve of St. John, and from then on Juan got cake and candles like the rest of the kids.

That is one of the sweetest, strangest scenes in *The Wave in the Mind*. It is also, if you look at it sideways, one of the saddest. A man who had lived his life without needing a birthday had to invent one because the American government could not understand a person who did not have one. The form had a blank, and if it wasn't filled the man did not officially exist. Not a problem of his. A problem of the form.

Le Guin worries that small problem into a huge one. The blank on the form is the same blank that shows up again and again in the story our country tells itself. It is the blank of the "empty" frontier, the "wilderness," the white space on old maps where cartographers painted sea monsters because they had not bothered to ask anybody who lived there. In all those cases, the blank is a lie. Somebody was always already there, keeping time in their own clock.

This essay is about what Le Guin does with that lie. Her argument, quietly stated and furiously felt, is that the frontier was never really a place. It is a line in the mind.

## Two Sides, Two Kinds of "Real"

Here is how Le Guin explains it in a piece called "On the Frontier." A frontier has two sides. Of course it does — any line does. But the weird trick of the word *frontier* is that only one side of it ever gets to use the word. You do not hear the Papago describing the Spanish conquistadors as their frontier. You hear the conquistadors calling the Papago *theirs*.

The side that calls itself the frontier is what Le Guin, borrowing from Chinese philosophy, calls the yang side. It is the side that pushes. It is the side with the flag and the shovel and the story about itself. On the yang side, the world ahead of you is empty. It has to be empty, or else what are you doing charging into it?

She gives us a line from Julius Caesar that ought to be taught in every history class in America: Caesar said he was not certain Britain existed until he went there. Think about that for a second. There were about a million people in Britain when Caesar showed up. They had names, kings, druids, roads, farms, babies. But from Caesar's side of the line, none of that counted as existing. It would start to exist the minute a Roman arrived to measure it.

The other side is the yin side, where you live. It is where your grandmother is buried and where you know which hill has the wild onions. It does not think of itself as a frontier because it is not going anywhere; it is already here. Then one day, out of what had felt like an ordinary forest or ocean, strangers come walking. And they tell you your world is new.

What makes Le Guin a great writer rather than just a clever one is that she refuses to treat this as ancient history. The line between the two sides is not on a map. It is in the mind. And the mind can draw it anywhere.

## Ishi Was Not Wild

The hardest page in this book, and maybe the most important, is about a single word: *wild*.

Ishi was a Yahi man who walked, starving, out of the hills of northern California in 1911, the last survivor of his people. Most of the Yahi had been murdered. The rest had hidden until disease and hunger finished the job. Ishi's mother died in hiding. He came out alone. Le Guin's parents were anthropologists; her father Alfred worked with Ishi until Ishi died of tuberculosis five years later, and her mother Theodora wrote the biography that most Americans know him by. The subtitle of that biography calls Ishi "the last wild Indian in North America."

Le Guin loves her mother's book. She hates that subtitle. And she is unsparing about why.

Ishi, she points out, did not come out of a wilderness. He came out of a world. That world had names for every stream, stories attached to rocks, a grammar, a cuisine, rules about how to bury your dead and how to greet a stranger. It had a history older and deeper than California itself. To call Ishi "wild" is to say that the people who killed his family and took his land were the civilized ones. It is to call the emptiness they created a wilderness, as if no one had emptied it.

The word *wild*, used this way, is a lie told by the people with the guns. It turns a crime into a condition. It turns the people your grandfather shot into scenery. And then, once you have reclassified them as scenery, you can feel sentimental about their "vanishing," as if it had simply happened, the way weather happens, and not as if anyone in particular had done it.

Le Guin is not melodramatic about this. She is angrier than melodrama. She is dry and exact, and that is worse for the reader, because you cannot dismiss her as upset. She has just been thinking about this for a long time, and she wants you to think about it too.

## Western Time and Indian Time

Remember Juan Dolores and his invented birthday? Le Guin, looking back, calls that her first encounter with the difference between what she labels Western time and Indian time. Not a lack. Not a failure on Juan's part to own a calendar. Just a different clock.

Here is where she quietly lands one of the book's big punches. A bureaucracy only has one clock. If you live by another — by the solstice, by the salmon run, by the year your sister was born rather than the numeral the pope assigned to it — the bureaucracy cannot see you. You have to translate yourself into its time, or else it files you under Does Not Exist.

That is what colonization of *time* looks like. It is the quiet cousin of the colonization of *land*. We notice when someone takes your mountain. We notice less when someone takes your calendar, because the calendar lives in your head. But the result is the same: your world gets filed under "past," or "primitive," or "not yet modern," and the new clock gets filed under "real." Your time is the rough draft. Their time is the final copy.

Once you see this, you start noticing it everywhere. Whole countries get talked about as "behind." The people who came up with GPS get called modern; the people who navigated the Pacific by stars for three thousand years get called ancient. Which one is older, really? Whose clock are we using here?

## Her Own Family, Held to the Same Fire

One of the things that makes Le Guin hard to dismiss as just another critic is that she is not pointing the finger somewhere else. She is a granddaughter of the American frontier. Her mother's family, she tells us, moved and bought and farmed and failed and moved on: Missouri, Wyoming, Colorado, Oregon, California, and back around again. *We followed yang*, she says, in one of the plainest and most devastating sentences she ever wrote. *We found yin.*

And her father — her beloved, undramatic father, who taught her to think — was an anthropologist who built his life on writing down the languages and lifeways of people whose worlds were being destroyed. Le Guin looks hard at this. She will not let him off. She calls what he did an act of imperialism. She also calls it an act of human solidarity. And then she refuses to resolve the two. Both things are true, at the same time, forever. That is what grown-ups sound like when they are telling the truth.

When Ishi died in 1916, someone in California decided to autopsy the body against Alfred Kroeber's wishes. Kroeber was in New York and sent a telegram that overruled science in the name of loyalty: do not cut the body, we stand with our friend. It came too late. The body was cut up anyway. Le Guin writes about this with controlled grief that is more powerful than any outburst would have been. Her father had lost his wife, his friend, and most of the people he had studied, and he had run out of words. He was silent, she says, because neither his science nor any other language he knew had words for what he knew.

Le Guin does the same thing with her own memories. She describes herself as a kid calling Juan "Lo, the Vanishing Papago," picking the phrase out of some terrible magazine article and flinging it at a man who was too patient to flinch. She does not excuse it. She does not pretend she was too little to know better. She holds it up to the light and says: this is what I did, and maybe he laughed, and maybe he didn't, and I would like to know for sure but the man is dead. That is what accountability looks like when it is not performance.

## Capitalism Needs New Empty Places

There is another move Le Guin makes in "On the Frontier" that is worth holding onto. She points out that capitalism, as a system, has to keep finding new frontiers. It has to. If it stops expanding, it stops working. So it has to keep inventing new El Dorados — new empty places to rush into, new blank slates to write our names on. When the land runs out, the frontier becomes the ocean floor, or the genome, or outer space, or your attention. The blank on the form moves, but there is always a blank.

This is why so much science fiction describes the future as an empty place. Humans fly into a universe where nothing has yet happened, and then happen to it. Aliens, if they show up, tend to be obstacles or monsters or romantic interests — never people whose home we are walking into uninvited. It is the same "the natives do not count" move that mapped the American West, just projected forward in time.

Le Guin hated this. In her science fiction, the future is not empty. It is crowded. It is older and larger than our present. We are the aliens in it. That is not a small reversal; that is the whole difference between her books and most of the genre. She refuses to write a future that is still playing the conquistador's game, just with better spaceships.

Her realistic fiction lives on the other side of the line too. It is about waitresses and librarians and housewives and the people who run the shabby motel on the edge of town. The ones the frontier is done with. The ones who got left behind when the wagons rolled on. That is where she puts her attention, because that, to her, is where most of the actual living happens.

## Why the Line in the Mind Still Matters

It would be easy to read all of this as history. A sad story about California, a long time ago, that we have now, mostly, outgrown.

Do not read it that way. Le Guin would be annoyed with you.

The line in the mind has not gone away. It has just moved. Every time a company talks about "disrupting" a market as if the market were empty before it got there, that is a frontier story. Every time a tech founder talks about "colonizing" Mars, that is a frontier story, and this time the word is not even a metaphor. Every time we talk about "developing" countries as if they were just earlier, cruder versions of us that will one day grow up into America, that is a frontier story.

The tell is always the same. One side gets called real, the other empty, and the empty side is said to live in an older, less finished time. The future belongs to the "real" side. The past is where the "empty" side is filed, permanently, so that no matter what they do they can never catch up — because by the time they arrive, the goalposts have already moved.

Le Guin's great gift is that she does not let us file any of this under "other people's problem." She is a settler's granddaughter writing about her settler family. She is an anthropologist's daughter writing about anthropology. She is a science fiction writer writing about science fiction's ugliest habit. She is standing inside the house she is criticizing, and she knows it, and she will not move out, and she will not stop pointing.

At the end of "On the Frontier," she names her inheritance soberly: not noble country, not ancestral wisdom, but the invasive grasses that settlers dragged onto the California hills and never took back. That, she says, is what her ancestors planted, and that is what she has to reap. It is a strange, proud, rueful passage. It does not forgive anybody. It does not ask to be forgiven. It just says: this is the ground I stand on, and I am going to stand on it honestly.

That is what she is asking us to do. Notice the line you have drawn in your own mind. Notice whose time you have decided is real. Notice whose side you have quietly assumed is the future. And then — gently, stubbornly, with the patience of a man letting a small child hold his finger all the way up a gravel driveway — consider that the other side was always there, living in its own present, with its own past behind it and its own future ahead, and that the only thing ever empty was your idea of it.
